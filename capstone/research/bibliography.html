Annotated Bibliography

Merino, Mariana, et al. “Body Perceptions and Psychological Well-Being: A Review of the Impact of Social Media and Physical Measurements on Self-Esteem and Mental Health with a Focus on Body Image Satisfaction and Its Relationship with Cultural and Gender Factors.” Healthcare, vol. 12, no. 14, 12 July 2024, pp. 1–44, pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11276240/, https://doi.org/10.3390/healthcare12141396.

This review goes over the connection between body image, actual body measurements, social media, and cultural and gender norms. It'll be useful for my project as understanding the psychology behind body image is crucial to my efforts to improve it for others. The author makes an important argument that these concerns need to be addressed at a the public health and social policy level, as these are systemic issues and must be treated as such. Similarly, I think education is so important for people to prevent learning misinformation about their bodies and about the other gender so as to have a greater knowledge and compassion for themselves and others.

team, NeuroLaunch editorial. “Pinterest and Mental Health: Exploring the Potential Risks and Benefits.” NeuroLaunch.com, 16 Feb. 2025, neurolaunch.com/is-pinterest-bad-for-your-mental-health/.

This website article lists some of the risks and benefits of Pinterest, as well as including suggestions for using it mindfully, moving forward. It'll be useful for my project as, in a way, I'm creating a mini social space which can lend itself to unpredictability and the potential to have negative impacts even when simple and positive in theory. I've used Pinterest as long as I've had access to the internet, and while I sometimes find myself feeling a bit jealous, I still come back to it as it's one of the more relaxing and slow-tempo forms of social media out there. This article agrees, and makes a good point that it may depend on the user's pre-existing mental health conditions or tendency towards perfectionism, as well as the age of the user, as kids and teens are more susceptible to the negative side effects. However, Pinterest feeds are quite simple to curate and, as the article suggests, keeping it positive and diverse is the best approach, along with setting screen limits and balancing inspiration found digitally with that of real life.

Van, Emily. “How to Love Your Body (Even If You Don’t Love the Way It Looks).” Emily van Eck Nutrition & Wellness, 7 May 2025, www.emilyvaneck.com/how-to-love-your-body/. Accessed 14 May 2026.

This website article describes the complex nature that is self-acceptance, and how it's not only crucial for a person's wellness, but also a stand against the oppressive systems that instill harmful beauty standards. I chose this article as it essentially is an answer to the prompt I'll be asking in my project, and I wanted to gain insight on the kind of response it may recieve.The author makes a good point that many struggle with being nice to ourselves because we're afraid of being selfish or self-centered, when really, it's doing more harm than good not to. I agree, I've learned that the higher my self esteem and the more I love myself, the kinder and compassionate I am with those I care about.
